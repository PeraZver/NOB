# 7th Banija Assault Brigade "Vasilj Gaćeša"

## Formation
 - **Date of formation**: September 2, 1942
 - **Place of formation**: Moslavina
 - **Constituent battalions**: 
   - Third Battalion
   - Fifth Battalion
   - Assault Battalion of the Banija Partisan Detachment
 - **Strength at the time**: Approximately 850 fighters
 - **Commander**: Nikola Maraković Nina
 - **Commissar**: Đuro Čizmek

## Operations

The 7th Banija Assault Brigade "Vasilj Gaćeša" was formed on September 2, 1942, in Moslavina, from fighters of the Third, Fifth, and Assault Battalions of the Banija Partisan Detachment. At its inception, the brigade had around 850 fighters, equipped with 554 rifles, 19 light machine guns, and two heavy machine guns. The brigade was named in honor of Vasilj Gaćeša, a commander of the Banija Partisan Detachment and a national hero who was killed in action against the Ustaše on April 29, 1942.

### Integration into the 7th Banija Division
From November 22, 1942, until the end of World War II, the brigade was part of the 7th Banija Assault Division. In September 1943, the brigade was renamed the First Brigade of the 7th Banija Assault Division, reflecting its prominent role within the division.

### Recognition and Honors
In January 1944, the brigade was officially designated as an "assault" unit, a title that recognized its effectiveness and valor in combat. Throughout the National Liberation War, the brigade was awarded several honors for its contributions, including the Order of Merit for the People and the Order of Brotherhood and Unity. In June 1958, on the fifteenth anniversary of the Battle of Sutjeska, the brigade was further honored with the Order of the People's Hero.

### Legacy
Fifteen members of the 7th Banija Assault Brigade were declared People's Heroes of Yugoslavia, highlighting the brigade's significant impact and the bravery of its fighters. Despite the incomplete documentation of its entire operational history, the brigade's legacy as a formidable and honored unit of the Yugoslav Partisan movement remains well-recognized.
